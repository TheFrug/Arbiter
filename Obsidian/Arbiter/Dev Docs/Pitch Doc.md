# Arbiter - Pitch Document

## The Game

- Title: Arbiter
- Genre: Confession Extraction Simulator/Narrative-driven puzzle simulation game
- Development Environment: Unity
- Most Unique Thing: Interrogate suspects with varied methods meaning varied conversations.
- Context: In Arbiter, you work as part of a dystopian police state to interrogate suspects to make them deliver a confession.
- Describe an example of play: During an interview with an argumentative punk suspect, the player can choose their strategy, going for a warmer approach using their empathy to soften-up the suspect to draw out a confession, and can decide to lighten or harshen the suspect’s sentence if they want to. 

## The Story

Arbiter is set in an ultra-capitalist dystopian police state where all civilian activity is monitored and judged on how it aligns with the state’s ever-shifting ideals. The last free and fair election was almost two decades ago, and since then the government’s grip on the nation has only tightened. The same man has been leader for years, though no one has seen him in some time, for he has been incredibly busy making the great nation a better place for all.

You play as Jean Rah, a young man who has grown up under this relatively new government. Jean inherited a strong code of morals from his family and earnestly believes the government aims to uphold them. When Jean was a teenager, his father was arrested on the grounds of ‘association with an anti-government terrorist group’. Jean’s family mourned their father’s loss while Jean, his soft mind filled with state-sponsored propaganda, couldn’t help but feel angry that his father endangered his family so recklessly, and developed a gratitude towards the state for its intervention. Jean would carry this loyalty through his life, eventually graduating high school and entering the workforce. Needing enough money to support both himself and his mother, Jean enrolled in the Arbiter program to begin working for the government, hoping to enact good by upholding the law.

Over the course of the game, both Jean’s naive loyalty to the state and ironclad trust in its justice system will be challenged by the suspects he encounters in his interviews. As his views are confronted, the player will guide Jean down one of two paths: swallow the propaganda and fulfill his role efficiently for the safety of himself and his family, or resist, using his small position to win escalating victories against the state.

## The Prototype

### Prototype Length in Minutes

The prototype version of Arbiter will consist of one in-game day, which will take around ten minutes. Each day will consist of multiple rounds of interrogations, with most of them being fairly straightforward, and some having more intrigue. 

### Mechanics/Systems Breakdown

Arbiter will be a highly dialogue-driven experience, with the game flow consisting of the player choosing what line of questioning to use and moving through branching dialogues, with some options causing RNG skill checks with multiple outcomes. A loose list of mechanics and systems that will need to be developed are as follows:

- Stat System + Character Creation sequence
- Audio Manager
- Yarnspinner Functionality:
- Skill checks integrated within Yarn scripts
- Custom OptionsPresenter to display skill check UI
- Unity Variable Manager 
- Interrogation UI
- Technique Selector system w/ Yarn integration
- Interrogation Form UI Manager
- Shift Manager to track multiple suspects and their associated interrogation forms.
### Story Section for Prototype

The prototype of Arbiter will depict one day out of the hypothetical series of days the full game would take place over. The specific day this game will cover is the first one with a suspect that really shakes Jean’s nerve. My current idea is that Jean encounters a rebellious youth who was clearly innocent of the crime that they have been accused of. This clear instance of the state’s ideology unjustly changing the course of this youth’s life will challenge Jean’s long-held misbelief that the state shares his moral code. This can open the door to further questioning of the system by Jean, or a doubling-down on his bias as a form of self-preservation. 

The day depicted should have a few bog-standard interrogations for players to get used to the interrogation minigame, before introducing the more difficult interrogation with the rebellious suspect. The player will ultimately get to choose how to document this unique suspect, signifying the primary moral dilemma of Arbiter.
